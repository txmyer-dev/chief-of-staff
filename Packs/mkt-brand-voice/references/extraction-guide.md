# Voice Extraction Guide

Read this when running Mode 1 (Extract) or Mode 3 (Auto-Scrape).

---

## Phase 1: Sample Assessment

Before extracting, assess what you have.

**Authenticity check:** Are these polished (website, press release) or raw (Slack, email)?
More polish = less authentic voice. Weight raw content more heavily.

**Variety check:** Do samples cover different contexts — professional, casual, educational?
Single-context samples produce one-dimensional voice guides.

**Exclusion check:** Flag and exclude:
- Platform formatting tics (LinkedIn line breaks, Twitter character forcing)
- Typos and autocorrect errors
- Phrases clearly borrowed from others (quotes, retweets)
- Unusually formal writing (legal, press releases)

**Size check:** Under 500 words total? Offer Quick mode (top 5 traits + 3 rules). Don't attempt full extraction.

Output a brief assessment before extracting:
> "I have [X samples / Y words]. Quality: [high/medium — why]. Using [full/quick] mode. Excluding: [what and why]."

---

## Phase 2: The Six Extraction Dimensions

Work through all six for a full profile.

### Dimension 1: Tone

Map where the voice lands on five spectrums:

| Spectrum | What to look for |
|----------|-----------------|
| Formal ↔ Casual | Contractions, slang, sentence fragments, colloquialisms |
| Serious ↔ Playful | Humor, lightness, gravity, wit |
| Reserved ↔ Bold | Hedging language vs. strong claims, confidence signals |
| Simple ↔ Sophisticated | Vocabulary level, idea complexity, abstraction |
| Warm ↔ Direct | We/they vs. I/you, personal stories, directness of address |

For each dimension, note a specific example from the samples.

### Dimension 2: Vocabulary

- **Signature words:** What terms appear repeatedly that feel distinctive?
- **Jargon level:** Heavy (assumes expertise), Moderate (uses terms but explains), Light (plain language), Translated (industry terms but always decoded)
- **Register:** Everyday words or elevated vocabulary?
- **Profanity:** None / occasional / frequent
- **Anti-patterns:** What words appear zero times that you'd expect? These are often avoidances.

### Dimension 3: Rhythm

- **Sentence length:** Short punchy (3-6 words), medium (10-15), long (20+)? Which dominates?
- **Fragments:** Do they use sentence fragments intentionally?
- **Paragraph length:** 1-2 sentences? 4-5? Variable?
- **White space:** Dense blocks or lots of breaks?
- **Lists:** Heavy list use or flowing prose?

### Dimension 4: Structure

- **Openings:** Bold statement, question, story/scenario, direct address?
- **Transitions:** How do they shift topics? Extract exact phrases.
- **Closings:** CTA, open loop, summary, punchy one-liner?
- **Headers/formatting:** Heavy structure or unstructured?

### Dimension 5: Personality

- **Relationship to reader:** Teacher, peer, guide, insider, challenger, cheerleader?
- **Self-perception:** Confident or self-deprecating? Polished or raw?
- **World view:** Optimistic or realistic? Urgency or patience?
- **Recurring themes:** What topics come up unprompted? These are what they actually care about.

### Dimension 6: POV

- **Person:** First person I, collective We, or mixed?
- **Reader address:** "You", direct name, "folks", "friends"?
- **Stance:** Is the writer always the authority, or do they invite dialogue?

## Phase 3: Phrase Harvesting

Pull exact phrases from the samples — don't paraphrase.

**Transition phrases** — how they shift topics:
> "Here's the thing...", "What I've learned...", "Let me be honest..."

**Emphasis phrases** — how they land a point:
> "The reality is...", "This is the part people miss...", "Full stop."

**Closers** — how they wrap up a thought or piece:
> "That's the move.", "Start there.", "Simple as that."

Aim for 3-5 examples in each category, quoted directly.

---

## Phase 4: Confidence Zone Mapping

One of the most underused voice tools. Map which topics they speak with full authority vs. earned perspective vs. curiosity.

| Zone | What it sounds like | Language markers |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Full authority | Expert territory — no hedging | "Here's what works.", "The answer is..." |
| Earned perspective | Real experience, not absolute mastery | "In my experience...", "What I've found..." |
| Active exploration | Still figuring it out, learning out loud | "I'm testing this...", "Early take:..." |

Assign their stated expertise areas to zones. A voice that sounds authoritative about one thing and exploratory about another feels real — a voice that sounds equally authoritative about everything feels hollow.

---

## Phase 5: Anti-Pattern Documentation

Source from evidence: "You never used [word] across [X samples] — it doesn't fit your voice."

What would they NEVER say?
- Words that would feel wrong in their mouth
- Phrases that would make them cringe
- Tones they naturally avoid
- Corporate jargon they seem to have deliberately avoided

---

## Phase 6: Self-Critique Checklist

Before producing the voice profile, check:

- [ ] Are the extracted phrases actually from the samples, or am I inferring them?
- [ ] Does the anti-pattern list include specific words/phrases, or just vague categories?
- [ ] Is the confidence zone mapping tied to actual topics, or generic?
- [ ] Is the tone spectrum based on evidence from samples, not assumptions?
- [ ] Could a ghostwriter use this guide without asking follow-up questions?

Flag any issues before finalising: "The anti-pattern section only has 1 entry — I need more samples or direct input."
