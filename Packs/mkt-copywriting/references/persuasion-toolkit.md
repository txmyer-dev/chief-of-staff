# Persuasion Toolkit

Practical writing techniques for persuasive copy. Load when generating any format.

---

## Headlines

Your headline decides whether the rest gets read. Most people scan it and move on — only the ones that earn attention get clicked. Always generate multiple and test.

### Core structure

> **[Action verb] + [specific result] + [timeframe or contrast]**

The contrast form ("weeks, not months") packs a before/after into a handful of words. The specificity form ("$47K in 90 days") creates immediate credibility.

### Five headline angles

**1. Outcome-first** — states the benefit plainly. No cleverness, maximum clarity.

**2. Curiosity gap** — teases a result or discovery without revealing it. Works because people can't leave an unanswered question alone. Must be specific enough to be believable.

**3. Social proof** — leads with a number or recognisable name. Borrowed authority. "2,000+ teams switched in Q1" works because the reader thinks "that many people can't be wrong."

**4. Contrarian** — challenges something the reader assumed was true. Only works when the challenge is genuinely surprising, not just edgy for its own sake.

**5. Story** — implies a narrative arc in one line. Setup, tension, implied payoff. The reader needs to know what happened next.

### Headline failures

- Prioritising wit over clarity
- Missing the reader's self-interest entirely
- Making claims too vague to picture
- Revealing so much there's nothing left to discover

## Opening Lines

The first sentence exists to earn the second. Nothing else matters about it.

**Patterns that work:**

- **Challenge** — state something the reader suspects but hasn't articulated. Creates instant alignment.
- **Scene-setting** — drop them into a moment. Date, place, detail. They're in the story before they realise it's copy.
- **Admission** — share a failure or struggle. Vulnerability disarms scepticism because it signals honesty.
- **Data point** — lead with a specific, surprising number. Credibility first, context second.
- **Question** — ask something that mirrors their internal monologue. If the question matches their reality, they're hooked.
- **Two-word sentence** — almost zero friction to start reading. Momentum builds from there.

**Openings that signal laziness:** Anything that could apply to any product, any audience, any industry. "In today's world..." is the worst offender. If you can swap in a different product name and the sentence still works, it's too generic.

## Curiosity and Open Loops

The brain craves closure. Mention something without explaining it, and the reader has to keep going.

**The gap:** state that something interesting exists, be specific enough that it sounds real, but hold back the answer. "I tested dozens of approaches. One outperformed the rest by a wide margin." — the reader needs to know which one.

**Transition hooks** (use sparingly, 2-4 per page):
- Phrases that bridge paragraphs by promising what comes next
- End a section with a hint about the next section
- Reference something you'll explain shortly

**Closing loops:** every open question must get answered. Teasing something you never deliver destroys trust permanently. Small loops close within a few paragraphs. Large loops close by the end of the piece.

## Keeping Them Reading

Once they start, the goal is to make stopping feel harder than continuing.

**Bridge phrases** — short connector words between paragraphs that reduce the friction of starting a new thought. "Look." "Here's the thing." "Turns out." They smooth transitions without the reader noticing.

**Repetition links** — ending one paragraph with a word that opens the next. It stitches sections together so there's no natural stopping point.

**First sentences stay short** — the opening line of each section should take almost no effort to read. Once they're in, you can expand.

**Rhythm variation** — monotonous paragraph lengths put people to sleep. Alternate between short punches and longer passages that breathe. The eye moves naturally when the shape of the text keeps changing.

**What kills momentum:** jargon that forces a pause, walls of text with no visual breaks, tangents unrelated to the main thread, repeating the same sentence structure five times in a row.

## Making Pain Concrete

Abstract problems feel overwhelming. Specific problems feel solvable.

Instead of describing a frustration, put a number on it. Add up the hours, the cost, the missed opportunities. When the reader sees a quantified problem, they instinctively weigh it against the price of your solution. You've turned a feeling into a calculation.

The other approach: describe a scenario they've actually lived. Not "communication is hard" but "you send a reply, then your colleague sends a different reply, and now the client thinks nobody's in charge." They feel it because they've been there.

## Digging to the Real Benefit

Features don't sell. Neither do surface-level benefits. For every feature, keep asking "and what does that actually mean for them?" until you reach something the reader would describe to a friend. Usually takes three levels:

Functional (what it does) → Financial (what it saves or earns) → Emotional (how it makes them feel)

Write from the deepest level. Not "saves time" but "you actually leave the office before dark." Not "automates outreach" but "you wake up to booked calls instead of an empty calendar."

## The Founder Story

For creator-led brands, the personal story is the highest-trust element on the page. The arc: where they started (relatable), what went wrong (honest), what they discovered (credible), where they are now (proof), and the offer (bridge).

Self-deprecation works because it signals the person isn't performing. Specific numbers work because they're verifiable. The implicit message: "I was where you are, I found something that worked, here it is."

If writing for a founder, always get their story. Don't fabricate or embellish — authenticity is the whole point.

---

## Using Proof

Vague endorsements ("Great product!") persuade nobody. Proof needs structure:

**For testimonials:** who they were before, what they did, what happened, how long it took, how they felt about it. The specifics are everything — "4 months" is believable, "helped me succeed" is not.

**For authority:** if recognised names have used or endorsed the product, lead with them. Borrowed credibility transfers instantly.

**For data:** real metrics, specific percentages, concrete before/after numbers. Round numbers feel made up; odd numbers feel measured.

## Calls to Action

The button text should describe what they get, not what they do. "Start building" beats "Sign up." "See the full breakdown" beats "Learn more." The CTA names the reward.

Below the button, reduce friction: state the guarantee, the price, how many others have done this, and how fast it works. Pattern: **risk removal + peer validation + ease of starting**.

---

## Writing That Sounds Real

Readers spot manufactured copy immediately. Signals that you're a real person writing for real people:

- Sharing specific numbers that a PR team would never approve
- Acknowledging where your product falls short
- Using language your audience uses with each other, not corporate speak
- Breaking grammar rules occasionally because that's how people actually talk

---

## AI Patterns to Strip Out

`tool-humanizer` handles this systematically, but be aware during drafting:

**Vocabulary tells:** words like "delve", "comprehensive", "leverage", "crucial", "unlock", "streamline", "landscape" — these flag AI to anyone paying attention.

**Phrase tells:** "In today's fast-paced world", "It's important to note", "When it comes to", "Whether you're a... or a..." — filler that sounds like padding because it is.

**Structural tells:** every paragraph the same length, every bullet starting identically, excessive headings, bold on every third word, numbered lists where order doesn't matter.

**Voice tells:** pervasive passive voice, no first/second person, constant hedging ("may potentially"), no contractions, perfectly grammatical but lifeless prose.

## The Landing Page Arc

When building a full page, this sequence covers the complete persuasion journey:

1. **Hook** — headline with a specific outcome or timeframe
2. **Problem** — make the pain concrete and quantified
3. **Agitate** — a scenario or story that makes it vivid
4. **Credibility** — founder story, endorsements, or proof numbers
5. **Solution** — the product framed as a transformation, not a feature set
6. **Proof** — testimonials with measurable outcomes
7. **Objections** — FAQ or "this is/isn't for you" section
8. **Offer** — pricing with clear value justification
9. **Urgency** — only when authentic (real deadline, real scarcity)
10. **Final CTA** — benefit-oriented, friction reducers underneath

Not every page needs all ten. But this is the full arc when the situation calls for it.
