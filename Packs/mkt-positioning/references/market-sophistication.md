# Market Sophistication Stages

Based on Eugene Schwartz's awareness levels. The market stage determines which angle type will cut through. Using the wrong angle type for the stage is the most common positioning mistake.

---

## Stage 1: New Category

The market hasn't seen this before. No competitors. No awareness of the solution.

**Angle approach:** Simple, direct announcement. Name the problem and the solution.
> "Now you can [do thing that wasn't possible before]."

**Messaging style:** Educate. Explain the category. Don't differentiate — there's nothing to differentiate from yet.

**Example:** The first email marketing tool didn't need to explain why it was better than other email tools. It just needed to explain why email marketing exists.

---

## Stage 2: Growing Awareness

Competition exists. Market is warming to the category. Early adopters are in, mainstream is considering.

**Angle approach:** Claim superiority on a dimension that matters.
> "The fastest / easiest / most complete way to [outcome]."

**Messaging style:** Bigger, better, faster. Superlatives work here because the market is still forming opinions. First-mover advantage in claims.

**Warning:** This stage is temporary. The moment competitors match your claims, you've entered Stage 3.

---

## Stage 3: Crowded Market

Many players. Similar claims. Skepticism rising. "Save time with AI" is now meaningless because everyone says it.

**Angle approach:** Explain the mechanism. Show WHY your approach works when others don't.
> "Here's WHY this works when others don't: [mechanism]."

**Messaging style:** Proof and differentiation. The claim alone isn't enough — the audience needs to understand the how. Named methods, proprietary processes, and unique mechanisms become essential.

**This is where most markets live.** If the market has 5+ competitors making similar promises, you're here.

---

## Stage 4: Jaded Market

Market has seen everything. Every claim has been made. Every promise has been broken by someone. High skepticism.

**Angle approach:** Identity and belonging. Stop selling the product and start selling membership in a group.
> "For people who [identity marker]."

**Messaging style:** Tribal. Who is this for (and who is it NOT for)? The product becomes a signal of identity rather than a solution to a problem. Exclusion is as powerful as inclusion.

**Example:** Apple doesn't sell computers to people who need computers. They sell to "the crazy ones" who "think different."

---

## Stage 5: Iconic / Established

Category leaders exist. Brand loyalty is high. New entrants struggle on features alone.

**Angle approach:** Exclusive access, movement, or paradigm shift.
> "Join the [tribe/movement]."
> "The [new paradigm] that makes [old approach] obsolete."

**Messaging style:** Either attach to the incumbent's gravity ("works with [leader]") or create a genuinely new frame that makes the entire category feel outdated.

---

## How to Assess the Stage

Ask these diagnostic questions:

1. **How many competitors can the customer name?** (0 = Stage 1, 1-3 = Stage 2, 4+ = Stage 3-4)
2. **What claims are they hearing from competitors?** (If all claims sound the same, Stage 3+)
3. **Has the customer tried and failed with alternatives?** (Yes = Stage 3-4)
4. **Do customers buy based on brand/identity, not features?** (Yes = Stage 4-5)
5. **Is there a dominant leader everyone compares to?** (Yes = Stage 5)

The stage isn't fixed — different audience segments can be at different stages. A technical audience might be at Stage 3 while a mainstream audience is at Stage 2 for the same product.

---

## Stage → Angle Type Mapping

| Stage | Primary angle types | Avoid |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | Simple announcement, Transformation | Differentiation claims (nothing to differentiate from) |
| 2 | Superiority claims, Speed/Ease | Mechanism explanation (overkill) |
| 3 | Unique Mechanism, Specificity, Contrarian | Simple claims (already saturated) |
| 4 | Enemy, Identity/Specificity, Risk Reversal | Feature comparison (audience is past that) |
| 5 | Movement, Paradigm Shift, Social Proof | Direct competition with the leader on their terms |
